Abortion in America

Roe V. Wade

By Ariane Evans, Abhishek Kulkarni, Muhammed Constantino

Legal Analysis

In 1973, the Supreme Court of the United States issued a ruling the case of Roe V. Wade. Roe was the pseudonym of a Texas woman whose real name was Norma McCorvey. Norma was pregnant with her third child, and as she lived in Texas was not able to get a legal abortion. Through means of meeting an adoption lawyer, Norma decided to sue her local district attorney, Henry Wade. This case got all the way up to the Supreme Court, and it a 7-2 vote issued a ruling that stated under the 14th Amendment of the United State Constitution, a woman had a ‘right to privacy’ granted by the Due Process Clause, granting her the right to deal with her pregnancy in whichever way she saw fit. The ruling also came with a frame work by which the States were to use. In the first trimester, the states could make no law banning abortion for any reason. In the second trimester the government could require ‘reasonable health regulations’. In the third trimester the States were at liberty to ban abortions entirely, unless the life of the mother or baby were in danger.

Influence of Ruling

This ruling was a blow to those states who wished to outlaw abortion entirely, and since they could not disobey the Supreme Court, they found other ways of preventing abortions. In states such as Mississippi and Alabama, the medical boards would decertify doctors who administered abortions, shutting down their clinics. Some states would end up with no abortion clinics at all, making it impossible for women to access abortions.
